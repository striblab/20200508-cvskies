Nights got darker

Among the initial effects of coronavirus restrictions in China was dimmer city lights, as noticed by researchers at both NASA and MIT.

By analyzing readings from NOAA and NASA's VIIRS satellite, which measures nighttime radiance from artificial light sources, it was possible to see where areas around Wuhan and other cities darkened.

Comparing VIIRS readings of the Twin Cities metro area across March compared to previous averages for the month suggests a loss of nightly radiance as Minnesota started shutting down.

While reduced activity at nights leads to less lighting – and therefore less skyglow – just looking at these satellite images alone can be deceptive since environmental conditions, including snow, can alter how light is reflected.

An analysis of the clearest VIIRS snapshots from multiple clear nights in February, March and April points to darker nights than average around Minneapolis, St. Paul and its suburbs during lockdown.

Snowfall this March wasn't too different from 2019, and the changes in lighting, while subtle, were consistetly dimmer than average as businesses started closing or keeping shorter hours while residents stayed home.

The International Monetary Fund found that nighttime lights are a close proxy for economic activity, something NASA is currently backing new studies to better understand.

With businesses reopening in Minnesota this month, radiance levels are likely to keep intensifying again.
