How Minnesota's skies have changed during lockdown

By Jeff Hargarten
Star Tribune May 18, 2020

Since coronavirus lockdowns started slowing society's normal daily grind, data shows the skies above became cleaner, clearer and less crowded.

Using satellite imagery and tracking statistics from various sources reveals a few ways reduced human activity on the ground has changed what's happening above the clouds.

As quarantine rules start to ease in Minnesota and nationwide, these trends are likely to gradually reverse, though how quickly remains unclear.

Swipe through the maps below to compare conditions in Minnesota's airspace before and after quarantine orders were enacted.

Air traffic dwindled

Far fewer planes are soaring over Minnesota compared to earlier this year, as airlines reduce scheduled flights in response to plummeting passenger demand and public health guidance discouraging air travel.

Though many airports remain open and domestic travel is still technically unrestricted, a ban on international journeys, the shutdown of some airlines and fears concerning COVID-19's spread have kept Minnesotans and other Americans on the ground.

Using sampled snapshots of planes tracked by The OpenSky Network during the 7:00 p.m. hour shows noticably fewer flights overhead the first Monday of January than in May.

Another plane tracking website, FlightRadar24, showed thousands fewer flights over the U.S. between March and April.

The TSA, meanwhile, reports only around 200,000 passengers passing through airport security checkpoints each day last week nationally – down from about 2 million before the pandemic.

Nightlights dimmed

With many Minnesotans working from home and most businesses either shuttered or keeping shorter hours, Minnesota's cities and suburbs are giving off less light at night.

Slightly darker skies are a side-effect of the shutdowns, as shown by NOAA and NASA's VIIRS satellite observations comparing nighttime glow of the Twin Cities metro just before coronavirus alarms went into full effect in late February to early May.

The International Monetary Fund found that nighttime light emissions are a close proxy for economic activity.

Researchers at MIT found significant reductions in light emissions across Chinese provinces as quarantines started earlier in the year.

Whether or not more stars are visible to the naked eye in urban areas after nightfall is unclear.

Atmopsheric pollution decreased

The skies also became somewhat cleaner, due in part to fewer vehicles on roads thoughout the state.

Satellite measurements taken by the European Space Agency's Copernicus Sentinel-5P satellite tracks the volume of the pollutant nitrogen dioxide (NO2) in the atmosphere, excess of which is a byproduct of human activity.

Comparing pollution maps from late February to early May shows a noticable reduction in NO2 emissions above Minnesota, particularly around the Twin Cities metro area. The ESA, which has been measuring similar reductions elsewhere, attributes these changes to quarantines.

As businesses reopen and restrictions are scaled back under Gov. Tim Walz's revised orders, pollution levels are likely to revert.

For instance, traffic volume on Minnesota's roads has already been creeping back up toward normal levels, and emission levels are likely to rise with it.
