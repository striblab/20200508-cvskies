How Minnesota's skies have changed during lockdown

By Jeff Hargarten
Star Tribune May 9, 2020

Since coronavirus lockdowns started shutting down society in Minnesota and globally, the skies above are cleaner, clearer and less crowded.

Using satellite imagery from various space agencies and tracking data reveals a few ways a lack of activity below has changed what's happening above the clouds.

Air traffic has almost vanished

Far fewer plans are soaring over Minnesota compared to last year, as airlines reduce scheduled flights in response to plummeting passenger demand and public health guidance discouraging air travel.

Though many airports remain open and domestic travel is still technically unrestricted, a ban on international journeys and fears of COVID-19's spread have kept Minnesotans and other Americans on the ground.

Atmopsheric pollution has decreased

The skies are also somewhat cleaner, due in large part to reduced air traffic and fewer cars on the road, according to data from the European Space Agency's Copernicus Sentinel-5P satellite, which has been tracking COVID-19's impact on emissions.

Comparing worldwide pollution maps from January to late March – around when Minnesota's shelter-in-place order took effect – shows a noticable reduction in pollutants above the state.

March 29, 2020 ⇨

Night lights have dimmed

With many Minnesotans working from home and most businesses either shuttered or keeping shorter, earlier hours, Minnesota's cities and suburbs are less lit-up at night.

Darker skies are a side-effect of the shutdowns, as shown by quartley VERS satellite data comparing late 2019 to early April 2020. Whether more stars are visible in urban areas after nightfall is unclear.
