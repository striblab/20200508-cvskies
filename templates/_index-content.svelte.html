How Minnesota's skies changed during lockdown

By Jeff Hargarten
Star Tribune May 19, 2020

Since coronavirus quarantines started slowing society's normal daily grind, data show the skies became cleaner, clearer and less crowded.

Using satellite imagery and tracking statistics from various sources reveals a few ways reduced human activity on the ground changed what's happening above the clouds in terms of air traffic, light emissions and atmospheric pollution.

As shelter-in-place rules start to ease in Minnesota and nationwide, these trends are likely to reverse – and some already are in the process.

Explore the maps below to compare conditions observed in Minnesota's airspace before and after lockdown orders were enacted.

Air traffic dwindled

Far fewer planes are soaring over Minnesota compared to earlier this year, as airlines reduce scheduled flights in response to plummeting passenger demand and public health guidance discouraging air travel.

Though many airports remain open and travel is still allowed, the shutdown of some airlines and fears concerning COVID-19's spread have kept Minnesotans and other Americans mostly on the ground.

Using sampled snapshots of air traffic tracked by The OpenSky Network during the 7:00 p.m. hour shows noticably fewer flights overhead on a Monday in May compared to a Monday in January.

Another tracking website, FlightRadar24, showed thousands fewer flights over the U.S. between March and April.

The TSA, meanwhile, reports only around 200,000 passengers passing through airport security checkpoints each day in May nationally – down from about 2 million before the pandemic.

Nightlights dimmed, then brightened

With many Minnesotans working from home and many businesses either shuttered or keeping shorter hours, Minnesota's population centers started radiating less light at night as pandemic lockdowns took hold.

NOAA and NASA's VIIRS satellite measures nighttime radiance from cities, and shows the Twin Cities metro was far brighter in late February compared late March after shelter-in-place was instituted.

While environmental conditions can affect radiance measurements, which can fluctuate each night, historical data suggests March was much dimmer than average.

Comparing monthly radiance averages from 2019 to samples of 2020 VIIRS captures suggests city lights emitted closer to normal radiance in February, dramatically less in March and started normalizing in April.

Dimmer lighting at night reduces skyglow above cities, though NASA is currently backing new studies to better understand, among other things, the extent COVID-19 shutdowns have affected light pollution levels.

The International Monetary Fund found that nighttime light emissions are a close proxy for economic activity. Researchers at MIT, meanwhile, found significant reductions in light emissions across Chinese provinces as quarantines started earlier in the year.

With with businesses reopening this month, radiance levels are likely to keep intensifying again – though skies may remain slightly darker than usual since most typical nightlife activities are still cancelled.

Atmopsheric pollution decreased, then returned

The skies also became somewhat cleaner, for a while, due in part to fewer vehicles on roads thoughout the state.

The European Space Agency's Copernicus Sentinel-5P satellite measures the volume of nitrogen dioxide (NO2) in the atmosphere, excess of which is a byproduct of human activity as it's formed when fossil fuels are burned at high temperatures.

Comparing pollution maps from late February (shortly before things started slowing down) to late April (after a full month of shelter-in-place) shows a noticable reduction in average NO2 emissions, particularly around the Twin Cities metro area. The ESA, which has been measuring similar reductions elsewhere, attributes these changes to quarantines.

Yet as summer weather arrives and restrictions are scaled back, it seems pollution levels are already starting to increase again in tandem with normalizing traffic volume on Minnesota's roads.

A sample of Copernicus Sentinel-5P data from May 11 and 12 shows NO2 emission levels somewhat resurging.
