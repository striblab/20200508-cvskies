How Minnesota's skies have changed during lockdown

By Jeff Hargarten
Star Tribune May 9, 2020

Since coronavirus lockdowns started slowing society's normal daily grind, data shows the skies above are cleaner, clearer and less crowded.

Using satellite imagery and tracking statistics from various sources reveals a few ways reduced human activity on the ground has changed what's happening above the clouds.

Swipe through the maps below to compare conditions in Minnesota's airspace before and after quarantine orders were enacted.

Air traffic has dwindled

Far fewer planes are soaring over Minnesota compared to last year, as airlines reduce scheduled flights in response to plummeting passenger demand and public health guidance discouraging air travel.

Though many airports remain open and domestic travel is still technically unrestricted, a ban on international journeys, the shutdown of some airlines and fears concerning COVID-19's spread have kept Minnesotans and other Americans on the ground.

Using snapshots of 7:00 p.m. flight traffic from The OpenSky Network shows more planes over Minnesota last year than this May, over a month since shelter-in-place took effect statewide.

Atmopsheric pollution has decreased

The skies are also somewhat cleaner, due in large part to reduced air traffic and fewer cars on the road.

Satellite measurements taken by the European Space Agency's Copernicus Sentinel-5P satellite tracks the volume of the pollutant nitrogen dioxide (NO2) in the atmosphere, excess of which is a byproduct of human activity.

Comparing pollution maps from late February to early May shows a noticable reduction in NO2 pollutants above the state, and many other places as well, which the ESA attributes to quarantines.

Night lights have dimmed

With many Minnesotans working from home and most businesses either shuttered or keeping shorter hours, Minnesota's cities and suburbs are emitting less light.

Darker skies are a side-effect of the shutdowns, as shown by NASA's VIIRS satellite data comparing nighttime glow just before coronavirus alarms went into full effect in late February to early May.

Whether or not more stars are visible to the naked eye in urban areas after nightfall is unclear.
