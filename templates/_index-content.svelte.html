Coronavirus

How Minnesota's skies changed during lockdown

By Jeff Hargarten
Star Tribune May 28, 2020

Since coronavirus quarantines started slowing normal daily life, data show the skies became cleaner and less crowded.

Satellite observations and other data reveal how human activity on the ground changed what's happening above the clouds, especially air traffic and atmospheric pollution.

As shelter-in-place rules start to ease in Minnesota and nationwide, these trends are likely to reverse – and some already are in the process.

Air traffic dwindled

Far fewer planes are soaring over Minnesota compared to earlier this year, as public health guidance discourages air travel and airlines reduce scheduled flights in response to plummeting passenger demand.

Though many airports remain open and travel is still allowed, fears concerning COVID-19's spread have kept Minnesotans and other Americans mostly on the ground.

Using sampled snapshots of air traffic tracked by The OpenSky Network during the 7:00 p.m. hour shows noticably fewer flights overhead on a Monday in May compared to a Monday in January.

Another tracking website, FlightRadar24, showed thousands fewer flights over the U.S. between March and April.

Nationally, the TSA reports around 200,000 passengers passing through airport security checkpoints most days in May, about 10 percent of pre-pandemic levels. Agents usually see more than 2 million people daily.

Atmospheric pollution decreased

The skies also became somewhat cleaner, for a while, due in part to fewer vehicles on roads thoughout the state.

The European Space Agency's Copernicus Sentinel-5P satellite measures the volume of nitrogen dioxide (NO2) in the atmosphere, excess of which is a byproduct of human activity as it's formed when fossil fuels are burned at high temperatures.

Comparing pollution maps from February (shortly before things started slowing down) to April (after a full month of shelter-in-place) shows a noticable reduction in average NO2 emissions – particularly that big cloud around the Twin Cities metro area. The ESA, which has been tracking similar trends elsewhere, attributes these changes to quarantines.

These maps use a month of observations to average out weather anomolies and other daily fluctuations in the readings.

While some areas may have seen higher NO2 concentrations, the biggest reductions noticed by the ESA were over large cities, and deeper studies are seeking to better understand how COVID-19 has affected pollution.

Looking at May, as warmer weather arrives and restrictions are scaled back, it seems pollution levels are already starting to increase again in tandem with normalizing traffic volume on Minnesota's roads.
